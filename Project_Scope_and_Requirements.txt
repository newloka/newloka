New Loka Project Scope and Requirements

1. Purpose

New Loka is an open source health data management system for patient care, clinical operations, and later research. It must work across a wide range of deployments without changing the core system design.

2. Scope

In scope:
- A single clinician using a phone offline
- A small clinic operating as a local mesh
- A rural hospital with intermittent connectivity
- A multi-department hospital with strict internal silos
- A research federation with privacy-preserving exchange
- Local document ingestion from photos, PDFs, voice notes, and dictation
- Local encryption, local storage, and local identity management
- Secure sharing and transfer of patient records between authorized nodes

Out of scope for the initial core:
- Dependence on cloud services for basic operation
- Separate codebases for different deployment tiers
- Automatic commit of AI output without human review
- Patient data sent to external APIs by default
- UI-only access control that is not enforced by the core

3. System Philosophy

The system must follow these principles:
- Platform-agnostic core
- Platform-specific user interfaces
- Local-first operation
- CRDT-based data model
- Extensible by modules, not forks
- Polymorphic behavior driven by deployment context
- Security enforced at the data layer
- FHIR as the internal clinical data model

4. Functional Requirements

4.1 Core clinical data
- Store all clinical records as FHIR R4 resources.
- Support patients, encounters, observations, conditions, medications, procedures, ServiceRequest orders, DiagnosticReport results, ImagingStudy records, Specimen tracking, compositions, detected issues, audit events, and related resources.
- Preserve provenance for every record, including creator, timestamps, origin node, and sync metadata.
- Support soft delete semantics only.


4.2 Computerized Physician Order Entry (CPOE)
- Support ordering of laboratory tests, imaging studies, and other diagnostic procedures.
- Maintain order lifecycle: draft, requested, active, completed, cancelled.
- Support order priority levels: routine, urgent, stat.
- Associate orders with encounters and responsible clinicians.
- Store orders as FHIR ServiceRequest, DiagnosticReport, ImagingStudy, and Specimen resources.
- Provide audit trail for every order action including creation, modification, cancellation, and result attachment.

4.3 Lab and imaging configuration
- Support configurable lab departments: Radiology, Biochemistry, Pathology, Microbiology.
- Lab configuration is determined at login time based on the user's department and role.
- Enforce lab-level access control at the ABAC layer, not just the UI.
- A clinician in Biochemistry can only access biochemistry orders and results unless explicitly granted cross-lab permissions.
- Lab technicians have Create/Update permissions for results within their assigned lab only.
- Radiologists have Read/Create/Update for imaging studies and reports within Radiology.
- Pathologists have restricted access to tissue and cytology specimens and reports.
- Microbiologists access culture, sensitivity, and organism identification data.
- Cross-lab emergency override follows the same emergency override workflow as other sensitive data.
- Lab configurations are stored per-deployment and may be empty for T0 (solo clinician) deployments.

4.4 Storage and encryption
- Use local encrypted storage on every platform.
- Encrypt data at rest with strong symmetric encryption.
- Keep keys in device-local hardware-backed stores where available.
- Support a hierarchical key model for device, patient record, sync session, and audit signing.

4.5 Identity and access control
- Require authenticated users for all record access.
- Enforce ABAC at the core, not only in the UI.
- Support department and team based silos.
- Support emergency override with explicit reason entry and audit logging.
- Support offline-capable authentication.

4.6 Sync and mesh
- Support peer discovery on local networks.
- Support delta sync between authorized nodes.
- Support conflict detection and deterministic merge behavior.
- Never auto-resolve clinically dangerous conflicts such as medication changes.
- Preserve full provenance during sync, merge, transfer, and correction.

4.7 AI and document processing
- Run OCR, speech-to-text, and extraction locally or on trusted institutional infrastructure.
- Support handwriting extraction from photos.
- Support PDF ingestion.
- Support in-app dictation and voice note processing.
- Require human review before committing AI-extracted data.
- Store confidence and warning metadata with extracted resources.

4.8 Audit and safety
- Maintain an append-only audit trail.
- Sign audit entries cryptographically.
- Make the audit trail tamper-evident.
- Record access, edits, sync events, overrides, and corrections.
- Support retrospective review and escalation workflows.

4.9 User experience
- Make essential information reachable in three taps or fewer.
- Keep offline status visible.
- Surface critical alerts immediately.
- Provide patient lookup, chart review, quick entry, document ingestion, handoff, and admin workflows.

5. Non-Functional Requirements

- Must operate fully offline for the single-clinician tier.
- Must degrade gracefully when network or hardware capacity is limited.
- Must keep the same core logic across all tiers.
- Must be reliable enough for clinical use.
- Must be secure enough for regulated health data.
- Must be extensible without redesigning the core.
- Must be testable in isolated modules and in end-to-end tiered deployments.

6. Deployment Requirements

6.1 T0 - Single clinician
- Runs on a phone.
- Uses local encrypted storage.
- Does not require a server.
- Supports offline workflow end to end.

6.2 T1 - Small clinic
- Supports LAN discovery and mesh sync.
- Can use a local hub or peer-to-peer exchange.
- Supports shared administration and local policy control.

6.3 T2 - Rural hospital
- Supports a local server with intermittent cloud sync.
- Preserves operation during network outages.
- Supports department-aware access controls.

6.4 T3 - Multi-department hospital
- Supports institutional access control and silo enforcement.
- Supports integration with hospital systems and internal FHIR services.
- Supports secure handoff between departments and teams.

6.5 T4 - Research federation
- Supports privacy-preserving data sharing.
- Supports consent-driven access.
- Supports federated or aggregate research workflows without exposing raw patient data by default.

7. Standards and Compliance

- FHIR R4 is the internal data standard.
- SNOMED CT, LOINC, and ICD codes are used where relevant.
- HIPAA applies where required.
- DISHA and the IT Act apply in India-facing contexts.
- GDPR applies in research federation contexts where relevant.
- Jurisdictional retention and export requirements must be respected.

8. Acceptance Criteria

- A clinician can create, find, edit, and review a patient record offline.
- A clinic can share records securely over a local mesh.
- A hospital can enforce department-level access controls at the data layer.
- AI-assisted ingestion requires review before commit.
- All access and changes are auditable.
- The same core system can be deployed across all tiers without forking.
